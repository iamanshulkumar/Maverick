## The Transition

I didn't plan to become a Project Manager. I was a Senior Full Stack Engineer who cared deeply about how the team operated. I asked questions like:

- "Why do we estimate in story points if we never check velocity?"
- "Who makes the call when engineering and design disagree?"
- "Why do we plan in two-week cycles when the business needs monthly releases?"

Eventually, I was given the opportunity to lead the engineering function at Yuvmedia. Here's what I learned.

## Lesson 1: Code Quality Doesn't Decrease

A common fear among engineers considering management is that they'll lose their technical edge. The reality is more nuanced.

In my first three months as PM, I wrote 40% less code than as an IC. But the code I did write was more impactful — infrastructure decisions, code reviews, and critical path work. My engineering judgment improved because I had to think about the whole system, not just my ticket.

## Lesson 2: Communication is the Force Multiplier

As an IC, your output is measured in code. As a PM, your output is measured in what your team ships.

I learned to:
- Write **one-paragraph summaries** instead of five-paragraph emails
- Use **async updates** (Slack, Notion) instead of scheduling meetings
- Give **direct feedback** in private, **public praise** in team channels
- **Repeat the mission** every week — teams forget why they're building

## Lesson 3: Let Go of Being Right

Developers are trained to be correct. Good PMs learn to be effective instead.

Sometimes the pragmatic solution wins over the architecturally perfect one. Sometimes the client insists on a feature you know won't work. Your job is to advise, document your concerns, and make the best of it.

## Lesson 4: Protect Your Team's Time

My single most important responsibility is shielding the team from context switching. Every interruption — a client call, an urgent bug, a status update meeting — costs 20+ minutes of productive engineering time.

I now require:
- All requests to go through me first
- Bug triage every morning (no ad-hoc Slack pings)
- Meeting-free blocks on Tuesday and Thursday

## What I Miss

I miss the flow state of writing code for hours. I don't miss the isolation of being heads-down on a ticket while the team drifted.

## What I Gained

Seeing five people ship work they're proud of, in an environment where they feel supported and challenged — that's more satisfying than any feature I shipped as an IC.
