## What Nobody Tells You

When I became Project Manager, I expected to do everything I did before — just with a team. Nobody told me that leadership would require unlearning most of what made me a good engineer.

## 1. Stop Being the Hero

Engineers become leads because they ship. But your team doesn't need you to ship their code — they need you to create the conditions where they can ship their best work.

My first month as PM, I wrote 2000 lines of code. My team shipped zero features. I was the bottleneck.

**Fix:** I stopped assigning myself tickets. I started doing code reviews, unblocking, and planning. Throughput went up.

## 2. Clear is Kind

Ambiguity is the enemy of execution. As an IC, ambiguity meant I had to figure things out myself. As a PM, ambiguity means five people figure things out five different ways.

Now I write everything down:
- **Weekly priorities** — top 3 goals for the team
- **Decision logs** — what was decided, by whom, when
- **RFC templates** — forcing structured thinking before implementation

## 3. 1:1s Are Not Status Updates

The most important hour of my week is the 1:1 with each team member. I don't ask "what are you working on?" — I ask:

- "What's been frustrating this week?"
- "What do you want to learn next?"
- "Is there anything I can take off your plate?"

## 4. Protect Your Team from Bad Process

Process exists to solve coordination problems. Too often, process persists long after the problem is solved.

Before adding any process, ask: "What specific problem does this solve, and can we solve it with fewer steps?"

I eliminated our three-hour sprint retrospective in favor of a 15-minute async form. The insights were better because people had time to think.

## 5. Lead by Teaching, Not Telling

When a junior engineer makes a design mistake, resist the urge to fix it yourself. Walk through the trade-offs. Ask questions. Let them arrive at the better solution.

It takes longer the first time. But the tenth time, they'll make the right call without you.

## The Bottom Line

Leading engineers is harder than engineering. It requires a completely different set of skills — communication, empathy, systems thinking applied to people instead of code. But the leverage is enormous. A good PM makes five engineers more productive than any one engineer could be alone.
